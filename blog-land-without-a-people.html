"A land without a people for a people without a land"

Where one of the most quoted, and most contested, phrases about the Holy Land actually came from.

History & narrative
By the VFHL editorial team · 7 min read

Few sentences have done more to shape how people imagine the Holy Land than nine deceptively simple words: "a land without a people for a people without a land." It has been quoted as fact, condemned as propaganda, and repeated by people on every side who had never traced where it came from. The real story is more interesting, and more instructive, than either camp usually admits.

It did not begin where most people assume

The phrase is often attributed to the early Zionist movement of the late nineteenth century. In fact, the words appear decades earlier, and not from Jewish nationalist writers at all, but from British Christian Restorationists, who believed Scripture pointed toward a Jewish return to the land.

One of the earliest clear uses comes from the Church of Scotland clergyman Alexander Keith in the 1840s, and a closely related formulation was popularized by the Earl of Shaftesbury, a prominent English evangelical and social reformer, in 1853. For these writers the phrase was woven into a religious and political vision of the era, long before modern political Zionism took shape.

The slogan was repeated far more often by its critics, as a thing to refute, than by the movement it is usually pinned to.

What the words were taken to mean

Read one way, "a land without a people" sounds like a claim that the territory was empty. It was not. Ottoman Palestine in the nineteenth century was home to a settled population of Arabs, both Muslim and Christian, alongside long-established Jewish communities. Scholars who have studied the phrase carefully, including Diana Muir and Adam Garfinkle, argue that many who used it did not mean the land held no inhabitants at all. To a Victorian ear, "a people" often carried the sense of a nation, a sovereign political body, rather than simply human beings present on the ground.

That distinction matters, but it does not erase the problem. Whatever the speaker intended, the sentence was easy to hear as a denial that the existing population mattered. And once a phrase can be heard that way, how it is heard becomes part of its history.

Why a 170-year-old phrase still does work today

This is the part worth sitting with. The argument over these nine words is rarely an argument about the 1850s. It is an argument about now. For some, the phrase stands for an erasure they still feel. For others, defending its "real" meaning is a way of defending a larger story about return and belonging. The history becomes a proxy for the present.

That is precisely the pattern we see, month after month, in our salons. People rarely disagree only about what happened. They disagree about which story the facts belong to. A single phrase can carry an entire worldview, which is exactly why pulling it apart, gently and accurately, can open a conversation that shouting never will.

How we read it

At Voices From the Holy Land we do not exist to win this argument for one side. We exist to help people understand it well enough to talk to one another. So our reading is simple:

  • The phrase is older, and more Christian in origin, than the popular version of the story suggests.
  • The land was inhabited, and any honest telling has to begin there.
  • What people mean by a slogan and what others hear in it are both real, and peace work has to hold both at once.

Understanding where a phrase came from will not settle the conflict. But it can lower the temperature of the room by a few degrees, and in a conversation this old, a few degrees is sometimes where everything begins.
